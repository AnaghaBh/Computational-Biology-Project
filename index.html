The standard model works at low density but predicts a band that is too narrow and sharp at high density. Results

What the model tells us

Result 1 — Experimental data

Bacteria form a moving band — but the shape changes with density

In the experiment, bacteria were placed in a long narrow channel filled with aspartate (a nutrient they like). They swam toward it, ate it, and formed a travelling band that moved along the channel. We measured the shape of this band at four different starting densities: LOW, MID, HIGH, and EXT.

At LOW density the band is narrow and sharp. At HIGH and EXT density the band is much wider and flatter. The band still moves at roughly the same speed in all cases (~0.28 mm/min), but its shape is very different. This is the key observation we need our model to explain.

Result 2 — Original model

The standard model works at low density but gets it wrong at high density

The Keller-Segel model describes how bacteria spread out randomly and also move toward food. We fitted it to the LOW density data and it matched well. But when we used the same settings for HIGH and EXT density, the model predicted a narrow sharp band — not the wide flat one we actually see.

The model is too aggressive: it assumes bacteria always chase the food gradient just as hard, no matter how crowded they are. The data show that is not true.

This diagnostic checks whether the model’s equations match the measured rate of change of bacteria. At HIGH and EXT density the model is far too large — confirming it over-drives the chemotaxis.

Result 3 — Fixed model

One small change fixes the model for all four densities

We changed one thing: instead of bacteria always chasing food at the same strength, we let that strength decrease when the bacteria are crowded. When there are lots of bacteria packed together, each one responds less strongly to the food gradient.

This single change fixes the model at all four densities at once. The wide flat band at high density is now reproduced, and the narrow sharp band at low density is still correct. One formula, four density groups, all matching.

With the fix applied, the model’s equations now match the measured rate of change at all densities.

Result 4 — Running the model forward in time

The model correctly predicts how the band moves over time

We took the real measured profiles as a starting point and ran the model forward for five minutes. The bacterial band moved to the right at about 0.28 mm/min — matching the speed seen in the experiment.

Behind the band, the food (aspartate) gets eaten up. Ahead of the band, fresh food remains. This is exactly what keeps the band moving: bacteria always have food to chase in front of them and nothing left behind them. The model captures this self-sustaining behaviour correctly.

Result 5 — What happens when antibiotics are added?

Bacteria can be attracted toward antibiotics — and it disrupts the band

Some bacteria (like Pseudomonas aeruginosa) have been shown to swim toward antibiotics rather than away from them. We added this behaviour to our model and placed an antibiotic source ahead of the bacterial band.

Below a certain concentration (the MIC, or minimum inhibitory concentration) the bacteria barely notice the antibiotic and the band moves normally. Above the MIC, bacteria at the front of the band swim into the antibiotic zone and get killed. The band slows down and starts to break apart.

Interestingly, at high density the bacteria are less attracted to the antibiotic (because crowding reduces their response to any gradient). This means a dense colony is actually harder to disrupt this way — an unexpected side effect of the density-dependent behaviour we found in Result 3.

The bacterial band (green) moves right until it hits the antibiotic zone (red), where bacteria are killed and the band breaks down.
